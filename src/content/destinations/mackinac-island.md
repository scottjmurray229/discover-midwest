## Discovering Mackinac Island

Mackinac Island sits in the Straits of Mackinac between Lake Huron and Lake Michigan, connected to the mainland by a 15-minute ferry ride and separated from modernity by a car ban that has been in effect since 1898. This is the longest-standing motor vehicle prohibition in North America, and it has preserved an island that looks and operates very much as it did in the Victorian era when the Grand Hotel was built in 1887.

The Grand Hotel's 660-foot porch is real — the longest porch in the world, lined with Adirondack chairs facing the Straits, with a view that includes the Mackinac Bridge's five-mile span connecting Michigan's upper and lower peninsulas. Breakfast and dinner are included in the room rate, the dress code for dinner is enforced (jacket required), and the whole experience is a sincere commitment to a style of hospitality that has mostly disappeared.

The eight-mile perimeter road circles the island perfectly for a bicycle ride. Arch Rock, a natural limestone arch 146 feet above Lake Huron, is the main non-hotel sight. Fort Mackinac dates to the Revolutionary War and offers living history programs in summer. And the fudge — yes, the fudge shops are genuinely excellent, and the island produces about 10,000 pounds per day in summer for reasons that have never been fully explained.
